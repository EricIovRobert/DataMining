\section{Description of the Dataset}

The Heart Disease dataset was collected by Janosi et al. \cite{janosi1989} and made available through the UCI Machine Learning Repository \cite{uci}. It aggregates data from four clinical sites: Cleveland, Hungary, Switzerland, and VA Long Beach. Only the Cleveland database has been used in published machine learning experiments, and it is the one used in this project. While the full dataset contains 76 attributes, only 14 are used in practice: 13 features and one target variable, across 303 records. The target variable indicates the presence or absence of heart disease. It was derived from an original ordinal variable with values 0 to 4, where 0 means no disease and any value above 0 was remapped to 1 \cite{uci}.

The 13 features cover a range of clinical measurements and can be grouped by type. Five are continuous: \texttt{age} in years, \texttt{trestbps} for resting blood pressure in mm Hg, \texttt{chol} for serum cholesterol in mg/dl, \texttt{thalach} for maximum heart rate achieved, and \texttt{oldpeak} for ST depression induced by exercise relative to rest. Three are binary: \texttt{sex}, \texttt{fbs} indicating whether fasting blood sugar exceeds 120 mg/dl, and \texttt{exang} for exercise-induced angina. The remaining five are categorical. \texttt{cp} encodes chest pain type with four values: typical angina, atypical angina, non-anginal pain, and asymptomatic. \texttt{restecg} describes resting ECG results. \texttt{slope} captures the slope of the peak exercise ST segment. \texttt{ca} gives the number of major vessels colored by fluoroscopy, ranging from 0 to 3. \texttt{thal} represents thalassemia type, with value 3 for normal, 6 for fixed defect, and 7 for reversible defect.

\subsection*{Missing Values}

The dataset is nearly complete. Only 6 values are missing across the entire dataset: 4 in the \texttt{ca} column and 2 in \texttt{thal}. All other features are fully observed.

\section{Description of the Processing Flow}

\subsection*{Data Preprocessing}

The preprocessing stage consisted of two steps: handling missing values and feature standardization.

As noted in the dataset description, 6 values were missing across the \texttt{ca} and \texttt{thal} columns. Since this represents only 2\% of the data, the affected rows were removed rather than imputed, leaving 297 records for further processing.

All 13 features were then standardized using z-score normalization, which transforms each feature to have zero mean and unit variance. This step is necessary because PCA, applied in the next stage, is sensitive to the scale of the input features. Without standardization, features with larger numerical ranges such as \texttt{chol} would dominate the principal components regardless of their actual relevance.

\subsection*{Feature Extraction with PCA}

Principal Component Analysis was applied to the standardized features to reduce dimensionality while preserving the most relevant variance in the data. PCA works by projecting the original features onto a new set of orthogonal axes, ordered by the amount of variance they explain.

Figure \ref{fig:pca} shows the explained variance ratio for each principal component, along with the cumulative curve. The first component accounts for 23.7\% of the total variance, and the contribution of each subsequent component decreases gradually. There is no sharp elbow in the curve, which indicates that the variance is distributed relatively evenly across the original features and that no small subset of components captures most of the information.

To retain at least 90\% of the total variance, 10 principal components are required, reducing the feature space from 13 to 10 dimensions. This reduction reflects the low redundancy among the original clinical features.
