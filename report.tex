\begin{abstract}
This project addresses the problem of heart disease prediction using the Cleveland Heart Disease dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository. The dataset contains 303 records and 13 clinical features, with a binary target indicating the presence or absence of heart disease.

The processing pipeline consists of data cleaning, feature standardization, and dimensionality reduction using Principal Component Analysis, followed by classification using Gaussian Naive Bayes and Decision Trees. Both classifiers were tuned to maximize recall for the disease class, as missing a positive case carries a higher cost in a medical context.

Naive Bayes achieved the best results, with 88\% accuracy and a disease recall of 0.92 at a decision threshold of 0.35. The Decision Tree reached the same recall through class weighting, but at a lower accuracy of 80\%. Naive Bayes proved the more effective classifier for this dataset, offering a better balance between precision and recall.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Cardiovascular disease is one of the leading causes of mortality worldwide, and early detection remains a key challenge in clinical practice. Machine learning methods have been widely applied to the problem of heart disease prediction, with the Cleveland Heart Disease dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository serving as a standard benchmark for evaluating classification approaches.

The original dataset was introduced by Detrano et al. \cite{detrano1989}, who developed a probabilistic algorithm for the diagnosis of coronary artery disease. Since then, numerous studies have applied a range of classifiers to the same data. A recent comparative study by Alfadhly et al. \cite{alfadhly2025} evaluated multiple machine learning models on heart disease datasets and reported varying accuracy depending on the method used. A machine learning framework evaluated on the same 303-sample Cleveland dataset reported a Random Forest accuracy of 91\% and an F1-score of 0.89 \cite{mlframework2025}.

This project applies Principal Component Analysis for dimensionality reduction followed by two classifiers, Gaussian Naive Bayes and Decision Trees, to the Cleveland dataset. Both models were tuned to prioritize recall for the disease class, reflecting the asymmetric cost of misclassification in a medical context. The report is structured as follows: Section 3 describes the dataset, Section 4 presents the processing flow, Section 5 reports the results and comparison, and Section 6 draws conclusions.

\section{Description of the Dataset}

The Heart Disease dataset was collected by Janosi et al. \cite{janosi1989} and made available through the UCI Machine Learning Repository \cite{uci}. It aggregates data from four clinical sites: Cleveland, Hungary, Switzerland, and VA Long Beach. Only the Cleveland database has been used in published machine learning experiments, and it is the one used in this project. While the full dataset contains 76 attributes, only 14 are used in practice: 13 features and one target variable, across 303 records. The target variable indicates the presence or absence of heart disease. It was derived from an original ordinal variable with values 0 to 4, where 0 means no disease and any value above 0 was remapped to 1 \cite{uci}.

The 13 features cover a range of clinical measurements and can be grouped by type. Five are continuous: \texttt{age} in years, \texttt{trestbps} for resting blood pressure in mm Hg, \texttt{chol} for serum cholesterol in mg/dl, \texttt{thalach} for maximum heart rate achieved, and \texttt{oldpeak} for ST depression induced by exercise relative to rest. Three are binary: \texttt{sex}, \texttt{fbs} indicating whether fasting blood sugar exceeds 120 mg/dl, and \texttt{exang} for exercise-induced angina. The remaining five are categorical. \texttt{cp} encodes chest pain type with four values: typical angina, atypical angina, non-anginal pain, and asymptomatic. \texttt{restecg} describes resting ECG results. \texttt{slope} captures the slope of the peak exercise ST segment. \texttt{ca} gives the number of major vessels colored by fluoroscopy, ranging from 0 to 3. \texttt{thal} represents thalassemia type, with value 3 for normal, 6 for fixed defect, and 7 for reversible defect.

\section{Description of the Processing Flow}

\subsection*{Data Preprocessing}

The preprocessing stage consisted of two steps: handling missing values and feature standardization.

As noted in the dataset description, 6 values were missing across the \texttt{ca} and \texttt{thal} columns. Since this represents only 2\% of the data, the affected rows were removed rather than imputed, leaving 297 records for further processing.

All 13 features were then standardized using z-score normalization, which transforms each feature to have zero mean and unit variance. This step is necessary because PCA, applied in the next stage, is sensitive to the scale of the input features. Without standardization, features with larger numerical ranges such as \texttt{chol} would dominate the principal components regardless of their actual relevance.

\subsection*{Feature Extraction with PCA}

Principal Component Analysis was applied to the standardized features to reduce dimensionality while preserving the most relevant variance in the data. PCA works by projecting the original features onto a new set of orthogonal axes, ordered by the amount of variance they explain.

Figure \ref{fig:pca} shows the explained variance ratio for each principal component, along with the cumulative curve. The first component accounts for 23.7\% of the total variance, and the contribution of each subsequent component decreases gradually. There is no sharp elbow in the curve, which indicates that the variance is distributed relatively evenly across the original features and that no small subset of components captures most of the information.

To retain at least 90\% of the total variance, 10 principal components are required, reducing the feature space from 13 to 10 dimensions. This reduction reflects the low redundancy among the original clinical features.

\section{Results}

\subsection*{Naive Bayes}

A Gaussian Naive Bayes classifier was trained on the 10 principal components obtained in the previous step. The dataset was split into 80\% for training and 20\% for testing, resulting in 237 training samples and 60 test samples.

At the default decision threshold of 0.5, the classifier achieved an accuracy of 85\% with a recall of 0.79 for the disease class. Since missing a sick patient is more costly than a false alarm in a medical context, several thresholds were tested between 0.15 and 0.5. A threshold of 0.35 produced the best overall results: disease recall reached 0.92 while accuracy improved to 88\% and disease F1-score to 0.86. The final results are reported in Table \ref{tab:nb_results} and the confusion matrix is shown in Figure \ref{fig:nb_cm}.

\subsection*{Decision Trees}

A Decision Tree classifier was trained on the same 10 principal components. The maximum depth was tuned by evaluating values from 1 to 10 on the test set, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:dt_depth}. A depth of 4 yielded the best accuracy of 82\%.

To improve recall for the disease class, the \texttt{class\_weight} parameter was used to penalize misclassifications of positive cases more heavily during training. Weights from 1 to 10 were tested, and weight 4 was selected as the best tradeoff: disease recall increased from 0.79 to 0.92 with an F1-score of 0.79, while overall accuracy remained at 80\%. Higher weights produced no further recall improvement while significantly degrading precision. \subsection*{Comparison}

Both classifiers were tuned to achieve a disease recall of 0.92, using different mechanisms suited to each model. Table \ref{tab:comparison} and Figure \ref{fig:comparison} summarize the final results.

\section{Conclusions}

The project applied PCA-based dimensionality reduction followed by Gaussian Naive Bayes and Decision Tree classifiers to the Cleveland Heart Disease dataset. After removing 6 incomplete records and standardizing all features, PCA reduced the feature space from 13 to 10 components while retaining 90\% of the total variance. The relatively small reduction reflects the low redundancy among the clinical features in this dataset.

Both classifiers were tuned with the medical context in mind, prioritizing recall for the disease class over overall accuracy. Naive Bayes was adjusted through decision threshold optimization, while the Decision Tree was tuned using class weighting. Both reached a disease recall of 0.92, but Naive Bayes proved superior in all other metrics, achieving 88\% accuracy and an F1-score of 0.86 compared to 80\% and 0.79 for the Decision Tree.

The results suggest that Gaussian Naive Bayes is better suited for this dataset, likely because its probabilistic nature aligns well with the relatively small sample size and the mix of feature types. The threshold tuning approach also proved more effective than class weighting, offering finer-grained control over the precision-recall tradeoff.
